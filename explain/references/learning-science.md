# Learning Science — Why This Works

---

## The Cognitive Mechanisms

This isn't just a communication style. It's grounded in how the brain actually encodes knowledge.

**Active recall** — Explaining forces the brain to retrieve information rather than passively recognize it. Recognition is cheap. Retrieval is what builds durable memory. This is why reading something twice feels productive but produces little — and why trying to explain it from scratch feels hard but works.

**Elaborative explanation** — Putting ideas into your own words forces you to connect new information to things you already understand. That connection is what creates a mental model, not a memorized fact. Isolated facts decay. Connected models compound.

**Metacognition** — The moment you try to explain and can't, you've identified a gap. That gap is more valuable than the explanation itself — it's the exact spot where real learning happens. The Feynman technique makes gaps visible immediately rather than hiding them behind re-reading.

**Chunking** — Breaking a concept into digestible logical steps reduces cognitive overload. The brain handles bite-sized reasoning far better than abstract wholes thrown at it all at once.

**Transfer learning** — When someone can explain a concept clearly, they're far more likely to apply it in a new context. Understanding the mechanism — not just the name — is what makes knowledge portable across situations.

Feynman's own distinction: *"There's a difference between knowing the name of something and knowing something."* Jargon is often the sound of someone who knows the name but not the thing.

---

## Research Backing

Empirical study across grades 4, 7, and 11 students showed that groups applying the Feynman Technique achieved significantly higher posttest scores and learning gains than control groups. The technique also shows strong results with slower learners — the active, iterative nature compensates for the slower initial processing.

The technique aligns with three well-established findings from cognitive science: active recall significantly outperforms passive review; elaborative interrogation builds more durable understanding than re-reading; and teaching others is one of the highest-retention learning activities (estimated 90% retention vs 10% for reading).

## Where This Works Best

Any topic that has a *mechanism underneath it* — a reason why it behaves the way it does. That covers most domains:

- **Programming** — async/await, memory management, database indexing, type systems, concurrency
- **Science** — how vaccines work, what a black hole is, why planes fly, how encryption works
- **Business and finance** — compound interest, market pricing, supply chains, unit economics
- **History and systems** — why revolutions happen, how institutions form, why certain policies fail
- **Everyday puzzles** — why the sky is blue, why bridges don't fall, how search engines rank results

The topic changes. The process doesn't.

## Where This Technique Has Limits

Be honest about these. Don't force Feynman style onto topics it doesn't fit.

| Situation | Why it's a poor fit | What to do instead |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Pure memorisation tasks | Vocabulary lists, multiplication tables, historical dates — there's no conceptual mechanism to unpack | Spaced repetition, mnemonics |
| Tacit / procedural knowledge | You can't Feynman-explain how to ride a bike, how an expert "feels" a good weld, or how a jazz musician finds the note — it has to be lived | Demonstrate, not describe |
| Already-simple concepts | If the idea genuinely has no depth, the full process is overkill | Just state it plainly |
| Highly abstract mathematics | Topology, category theory, advanced number theory — everyday analogies break down fast and can mislead more than they illuminate | Be explicit about where the analogy fails; don't pretend it holds further than it does |
| Real-time emotional situations | Grief, panic, interpersonal conflict — clarity and first principles aren't what's needed | Presence, not explanation |

---

## The Over-Simplification Trap

Being able to explain something simply doesn't guarantee you've captured its full depth. The research flags this explicitly: a learner can produce a simple explanation and *still* be missing the complexity underneath.

A good Feynman explanation teaches the true mechanism at the level *needed* — not the deepest possible level, but not a misleading shortcut either.

If the simplified version would lead someone to make a wrong decision in practice, say so explicitly. Flag where the real complexity lives. The explanation is only complete if the listener knows both what they now understand *and* where that understanding's limits are.
